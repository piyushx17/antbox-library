Chart of Accounts & Bookkeeping Foundations

Everything in finance starts here. The chart of accounts is the spine. Debits and credits are the grammar. Every financial statement, every report, every audit question eventually traces back to a journal entry someone made in a ledger. Get this layer right and the rest becomes navigation rather than wandering.

Diagram 01: The accounting equation
The single foundation. Every transaction the business ever does must keep this equation in balance. If it doesn't, something is wrong.
001

Chart of Accounts CoA · the master list

The structured list of every account the business uses to record its transactions, grouped under the five categories: assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses. Each account has a code (e.g., 1001 = Cash in Bank, 4001 = Sales Revenue) and a name. The CoA is the skeleton of the entire accounting system, every Tally, Zoho Books, or QuickBooks setup begins by defining it.

Example
A SaaS company's CoA might have 1001 Cash, 1101 AR, 2001 AP, 3001 Share Capital, 4001 Subscription Revenue, 5001 Salaries, 5101 AWS Hosting.
You'll see it
Every time you record a transaction, you select a CoA account. Wrong account = wrong report.
How to handle
Don't invent new CoA codes on your own. If a transaction doesn't fit an existing account, flag it to your manager, adding to the CoA is a deliberate decision, not a workaround.
002

Asset what the business owns

Anything of economic value the business controls and expects to generate future benefit from. Two flavors: current assets (turn into cash within 12 months, cash itself, receivables, inventory, prepaid expenses) and non-current assets (held longer, property, plant, equipment, software, goodwill). Assets sit on the left side of the balance sheet.

Example
₹50L in the company's HDFC current account is a current asset. A ₹40L office equipment purchase is a non-current asset (and depreciates over time).
You'll see it
Every balance sheet review.
How to handle
When booking a purchase, ask: is this consumed within 12 months (expense) or is it long-lived (asset)? Misclassifying CapEx as OpEx distorts both the P&L and the balance sheet.
003

Liability what the business owes

An obligation the business has to settle, usually with cash, sometimes with goods or services. Current liabilities are due within 12 months (AP, accrued salaries, GST payable, short-term loans). Non-current liabilities are longer-term (term loans, debentures, lease obligations). Liabilities sit on the right side of the balance sheet, above equity.

Example
A vendor invoice for ₹3L for laptops bought last week, due in 30 days, is a current liability sitting in AP.
You'll see it
Whenever you book a bill but haven't yet paid it.
How to handle
Liabilities recognize what you owe, not what you've paid. The day the invoice arrives is the day the liability is recorded, not the day you transfer money.
004

Equity owners' stake

The residual interest in the assets after deducting liabilities. Made up of share capital (what owners put in), reserves (transfers from profits set aside for specific purposes), and retained earnings (cumulative profits not distributed as dividends). Equity grows as the business is profitable and shrinks if it loses money. Goes to zero or negative if accumulated losses exceed paid-in capital, and that's bad.

Example
Founders put in ₹1Cr as share capital. The company makes ₹40L profit, distributes nothing. Retained earnings = ₹40L. Total equity = ₹1.4Cr.
You'll see it
On every balance sheet and in cap table discussions.
How to handle
Equity transactions (share issuance, buybacks, dividends) are sensitive. Never book one without explicit written approval, these have legal and tax implications.
005

Revenue top line

The income earned from the business's core operating activity, selling goods or providing services. Revenue is recognized when earned, not necessarily when cash is received (that distinction is what accrual accounting is about). Revenue sits at the top of the income statement and is the single most-watched number after cash.

Example
An invoice for ₹2L raised in March for services delivered in March is March revenue, even if the client pays in May.
You'll see it
Every revenue booking, every MIS report, every investor update.
How to handle
Don't confuse revenue with collections. Recording revenue based on payment received is cash-basis accounting; in accrual, you book it when delivered.
006

Expense cost of doing business

The cost incurred to earn revenue or operate the business, salaries, rent, electricity, software subscriptions, marketing spend. Expenses reduce profit. Recognized in the period they're incurred, not when paid (accrual principle). Expenses are also categorized as operating (OpEx) vs capital (CapEx), only OpEx hits the P&L directly; CapEx is capitalized and depreciated.

Example
March electricity bill of ₹40K received in April for March consumption is a March expense (accrue at March-end).
You'll see it
Every day. Most of your bookkeeping work will be expense booking and classification.
How to handle
Classify carefully, wrong account = wrong departmental P&L = wrong management decisions downstream.
007

Debit left side of the ledger

One half of every accounting entry. Debits increase assets and expenses; debits decrease liabilities, equity, and revenue. The word doesn't mean "negative" or "money going out" in the casual sense, it's a positional rule. Memorize: "DEAD", Debits go up for Expenses, Assets, Dividends. The rest go down on a debit.

Example
When you record salary payment: Debit Salary Expense ₹5L, Credit Bank ₹5L. Salary expense goes up (debit), Cash goes down (credit).
You'll see it
In every single journal entry, forever.
How to handle
If you're new, draw a T-account for every entry until the rules become reflexive. Don't trust your gut, trust the rule.
008

Credit right side of the ledger

The other half of every entry. Credits increase liabilities, equity, and revenue; credits decrease assets and expenses. Like "debit," the word is positional, not directional in the everyday sense. Memorize: "CLER", Credits go up for Liabilities, Equity, Revenue. The rest go down on a credit. Total debits must equal total credits in every journal entry, always.

Example
When you book a sale invoice: Debit AR ₹1L (asset up), Credit Sales Revenue ₹1L (revenue up).
You'll see it
Same as debits, every entry has both.
How to handle
If debits ≠ credits in your entry, you can't post it. The system will reject it. If a colleague's entry looks unbalanced, flag it.
009

Double-Entry Bookkeeping the universal system

The system where every transaction is recorded in two accounts, one debit, one credit, of equal amount. Used by every business globally for the last 500 years because it provides automatic self-checking: if the books don't balance, you know you made an error. Single-entry (just listing receipts and payments) is what a chai shop does. Double-entry is what an audited company does.

Example
Buy a laptop for cash ₹80K: Debit Computers (asset up), Credit Bank (asset down). Two entries, equal amounts, equation stays balanced.
You'll see it
Always.
How to handle
The system enforces it for you. Your job is to pick the right two (or more) accounts and the right amounts.
010

Journal Entry JE · the atomic unit

A single recorded transaction in the accounting system. Includes date, accounts debited and credited, amounts, narration (the explanation of why the entry was made), and supporting reference (invoice number, voucher, agreement). Every action you take as a finance associate eventually becomes a journal entry somewhere. Journal entries flow into the general ledger.

Example
"30 Mar 2026, Dr Salary Expense ₹10L, Cr Bank ₹10L. Being salary paid for March 2026, batch txn ID HDFC78921."
You'll see it
Hundreds of times a week during month-end.
How to handle
Always write a clear narration. "Being..." is the convention. Future-auditor-you will thank present-you.
011

General Ledger GL · the master record

The complete record of all financial transactions, organized by account. Every journal entry posts into one or more GL accounts. The GL is what financial statements are eventually built from. When an auditor says "show me the ledger for Sales Revenue, FY26," they mean every transaction in that account, chronologically.

Example
The Sales Revenue GL for March will show every invoice raised in March, totaling to the March sales number on the P&L.
You'll see it
In every audit prep, every variance investigation, every month-end review.
How to handle
Run the GL of any account where the trial balance number looks off, it'll show you exactly which entry caused the variance.
012

Sub-ledger the detail layer below the GL

A subordinate ledger that holds the detail behind a control account in the GL. The AR sub-ledger has one row per customer; the AP sub-ledger has one row per vendor; the inventory sub-ledger has one row per SKU. The sub-ledger total must reconcile to the GL control account total. If it doesn't, something is missing or duplicated.

Example
Total AR in the GL: ₹85L. Sum of all customer balances in the AR sub-ledger: ₹85L. If they don't match, you investigate.
You'll see it
In every reconciliation routine and audit prep.
How to handle
When investigating a mismatch, start with entries posted directly to the GL control account bypassing the sub-ledger, that's the most common cause.
013

Trial Balance TB · the first integrity check

A list of every GL account with its closing debit or credit balance as of a date. The total of all debit balances must equal the total of all credit balances, if it doesn't, there's a posting error somewhere in the books. The trial balance is the source from which financial statements are prepared. Closing the TB is the first milestone of month-end.

Example
March-end TB: total debits ₹4.2Cr, total credits ₹4.2Cr. Tied. Now we can prepare the P&L and balance sheet.
You'll see it
Every month-end, and any time someone says "let me pull the TB."
How to handle
If the TB doesn't tie, the most common causes are: one-sided entries, wrong account heads, or sub-ledger-to-GL breaks. Start with those.
014

T-Account the learning visualization

A simple two-column representation of an account, drawn like a T, debits on the left, credits on the right. Not a real software thing, but a thinking tool every accountant uses to work through complex entries. When you're trying to figure out how a transaction should be booked, draw the T-accounts.

Example
You're booking a vendor payment with TDS deducted. Draw three T-accounts (Vendor, Bank, TDS Payable) and work out the entries until they balance.
You'll see it
In your own notebook when you're learning, on whiteboards in audit discussions.
How to handle
Use them shamelessly while you're new. Senior accountants don't draw them because they've internalized the rules, but they got there by drawing thousands.
015

Contra Account the offsetting account

An account that offsets another account on the same side of the equation, typically used to net down without erasing the original. Accumulated depreciation is a contra-asset (offsets the asset); allowance for doubtful debts is a contra-asset (offsets AR); sales returns is a contra-revenue (offsets gross sales).

Example
Equipment ₹50L (asset) minus Accumulated Depreciation ₹30L (contra-asset) = Net Book Value ₹20L shown on the balance sheet.
You'll see it
Most often in fixed asset schedules, AR aging with provisions, and revenue reconciliations.
How to handle
Don't treat a contra account as a reversal, it's an offset. The original entry still stands, and the contra sits beside it for transparency.
02

The Three Financial Statements

Three documents tell you everything about a business. The balance sheet (what the company has and owes at a point in time). The P&L (how profitably it operated over a period). The cash flow statement (where the cash actually went, separate from profit). The three are connected. Change one, the others react.

Diagram 02: The accounting cycle
From the moment a transaction happens to the moment it appears in financial statements. Every step is a place where things can go wrong, which is also why every step has a reconciliation.
016

Balance Sheet BS · statement of financial position

A snapshot of what the business owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the residual to owners (equity), as of a specific date. The balance sheet is dated "as at", not "for the period." It must balance: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Pulled at every period-end (monthly, quarterly, annually) and is the single most-scrutinized statement by lenders and investors.

Example
"Balance Sheet as at 31 March 2026" lists assets, liabilities, equity at that exact moment.
You'll see it
Every month-end close, every investor update, every audit.
How to handle
If the balance sheet doesn't balance, stop everything else. A non-balancing BS means something fundamental is broken, not a minor rounding issue.
017

Income Statement (P&L) Profit & Loss

The statement showing revenue earned and expenses incurred over a period, ending in net profit (or loss). Dated "for the period from X to Y." Structured as Revenue minus COGS = Gross Profit, minus OpEx = EBITDA, minus depreciation = EBIT, minus interest and tax = Net Profit. Each layer tells you something different about profitability.

Example
"P&L for the year ended 31 March 2026" reports the full year's income and expenses.
You'll see it
Daily in MIS reports, monthly in close, quarterly for investors.
How to handle
Always show comparative numbers (this period vs prior period). A P&L without a comparison is half a P&L.
018

Cash Flow Statement CFS

The statement showing where cash actually came from and went, separate from accounting profit. Split into three sections: Operating (from running the business), Investing (CapEx, acquisitions, investments), Financing (loans, equity, dividends). A profitable company can still run out of cash, and the CFS is how you spot that before it becomes terminal.

Example
P&L shows ₹50L profit but the CFS shows negative ₹30L operating cash because AR ballooned by ₹80L. That's a collection problem masquerading as growth.
You'll see it
Monthly in management reporting, mandatorily in audited annual financials.
How to handle
The most important section is Cash from Operations. If it's consistently negative while the P&L shows profit, dig.
019

Statement of Changes in Equity SoCE

A statement showing how each component of equity (share capital, reserves, retained earnings) moved during the period. Captures share issuances, buybacks, dividends declared, profit/loss transferred to reserves. Less glamorous than the other three statements but mandatory in audited financials.

Example
"Opening retained earnings ₹2Cr + Profit for the year ₹40L - Dividend ₹10L = Closing retained earnings ₹2.3Cr."
You'll see it
In audited annual reports, in any equity transaction review.
How to handle
Tally to the prior year's closing equity and to this year's balance sheet equity. Differences are explainable line by line, or something's missing.
020

Accrual Basis Accounting when earned / when incurred

The principle that revenue is recognized when earned (delivered) and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands. Mandatory for all companies under Indian Companies Act and globally under IFRS / Indian Accounting Standards. Makes the P&L meaningful as a measure of business performance over a period.

Example
Subscription invoice raised in March for service delivered in April, revenue is April, not March.
You'll see it
In every revenue recognition decision and every month-end accrual entry.
How to handle
If a transaction crosses period boundaries, ask: which period does the activity actually relate to? That period gets the entry.
021

Cash Basis Accounting when paid / when received

The principle that revenue is recognized when cash is received and expenses when cash is paid. Simpler to maintain but loses the link between activity and reporting period. Allowed only for small, unaudited entities (e.g., professionals below a turnover threshold under the Income Tax Act). No serious business uses it for financial reporting.

Example
A freelancer reports income only when clients pay them, not when invoices are raised. Easy, but doesn't tell you how the year actually went.
You'll see it
Sometimes in personal tax returns, almost never in company financials.
How to handle
If a client is on cash basis and considering audit, they'll need to convert, be prepared for the cleanup.
022

Fiscal Year FY · the reporting year

The 12-month period a business uses for financial reporting. In India, the statutory fiscal year is 1 April to 31 March, referred to as FY26 for April 2025 to March 2026. US companies often use calendar year (Jan-Dec) or other periods. The fiscal year shapes deadlines for tax filing, audit, board meetings.

Example
FY26 ends 31 March 2026. Audit happens between April and September. Tax filing deadline is 31 October.
You'll see it
In every report header and compliance deadline.
How to handle
Know your client's fiscal year before you do anything, period-end work is meaningless against the wrong calendar.
023

MTD / QTD / YTD cumulative time periods

Month-to-date, Quarter-to-date, Year-to-date. Cumulative numbers from the start of the respective period up to a given date. Used in MIS dashboards, sales reports, and management reviews to track progress against targets within the period.

Example
On 15 March, "MTD Sales: ₹1.2Cr" means sales from 1 to 15 March. "YTD Sales: ₹14Cr" means total from 1 April to 15 March.
You'll see it
Every management review.
How to handle
State the as-of date clearly. "YTD" without a date is ambiguous and dangerous.
024

Comparative Statements period-over-period

Financial statements presented alongside the prior period's numbers for direct comparison. Indian Companies Act mandates current-year vs prior-year presentation in audited financials. Comparison reveals trends, growth, deterioration, a single-period statement is half-blind.

Example
FY26 P&L: Revenue ₹15Cr (vs ₹12Cr in FY25, +25%). The comparison gives the number meaning.
You'll see it
In every annual report and every well-built MIS.
How to handle
Restate prior period numbers if accounting policies changed in the current year. Otherwise the comparison is misleading.
025

Notes to Accounts the fine print

Detailed disclosures attached to financial statements that explain accounting policies used, breakdowns of major line items, contingent liabilities, related party transactions, and anything else needed for the reader to understand the numbers. Mandatory in audited financials. Often longer than the main statements.

Example
"Revenue is recognized as per Ind AS 115, at the point goods are delivered or services rendered." That's a notes disclosure.
You'll see it
In every audited annual report. Auditors care about this section more than most readers do.
How to handle
The notes are not boilerplate, they're audit evidence. Wrong disclosures = audit qualification.
026

Going Concern the survival assumption

The assumption that the business will continue operating for at least the next 12 months. Financial statements are prepared on a going concern basis unless evidence suggests otherwise. If there's material doubt (negative net worth, persistent losses, unpaid statutory dues), auditors must flag it, and that flag is the loudest warning in finance.

Example
An audit report with a "going concern" qualification is a public signal to creditors and investors that the company may not survive.
You'll see it
Rarely on a healthy client; loudly on a struggling one.
How to handle
If you spot signals (cash running out, vendor dues piling up), escalate. Going concern issues don't fix themselves.
027

Materiality the "would it change a decision" test

The threshold above which an error or omission would influence the decisions of a reasonable user of the financial statements. Material items are presented separately, immaterial ones can be grouped. There's no fixed number, auditors use rules of thumb (e.g., 5% of profit before tax, or 1% of revenue) but it's judgment-based.

Example
A ₹500 mis-posting on a ₹50Cr company is immaterial; a ₹5Cr revenue mis-statement is material and must be corrected.
You'll see it
In every audit conversation about adjustments.
How to handle
Materiality is no excuse for sloppiness. Small errors compound. Fix what you can; flag what you can't.
Diagram 03: How the three statements connect
Net profit from the P&L flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet. Operating cash flow ties back to changes in working capital items on the BS. The three statements are not independent: they're three views of the same business.
When you're learning, build all three for the same business from the same data, that's the moment the system clicks. Until then, it's just three documents.
03

Revenue & Accounts Receivable

Revenue is where careful judgment lives. The same transaction can be revenue now, revenue later, deferred revenue, or unbilled revenue depending on when the work happens, when the invoice goes out, and when the cash arrives. AR is what's left dangling, the promise that the customer will pay. Track it tightly or watch it become a problem.

Diagram 04: Revenue recognition timing
The same ₹1.2L contract sits in four different places on the balance sheet over its life depending on which event has happened: invoice, service delivery, payment.
If you bill annually upfront and deliver monthly (SaaS pattern), each month you reclassify 1/12 of deferred revenue into earned revenue. Mess this up and you'll either inflate or understate your revenue.
028

Revenue Recognition when to call it revenue

The rules that determine when revenue earned from a contract gets booked on the P&L. Under Ind AS 115 / IFRS 15, revenue is recognized when control of the goods or service transfers to the customer. For SaaS, that's typically over the subscription period. For services, when delivered. For goods, when shipped or received. Getting this wrong is one of the most common audit findings.

Example
A ₹12L annual SaaS contract billed upfront is recognized at ₹1L/month over 12 months, not ₹12L on Day 1.
You'll see it
Every time someone raises an upfront invoice for ongoing service.
How to handle
Default to spreading revenue over the delivery period unless the contract explicitly says otherwise. When in doubt, ask senior finance.
029

Accrued Revenue earned but not yet invoiced

Revenue that has been earned (service delivered) but not yet billed to the customer as of period-end. Recognized as revenue on the P&L and as a current asset ("accrued income" or "unbilled receivables") on the balance sheet. Reverses out when the invoice is eventually raised.

Example
A consulting engagement runs through March; invoice gets raised on 5 April. At March-end you accrue the March portion.
You'll see it
At every month-end, especially in services businesses.
How to handle
Reverse the accrual in the new month when you book the actual invoice. Otherwise you'll double-count revenue.
030

Deferred Revenue billed in advance, not yet earned

Cash collected (or invoice raised) for services or goods to be delivered in future periods. Sits as a liability on the balance sheet, the company owes the customer the service. As delivery happens, it's progressively reclassified into revenue. Very common in SaaS, insurance, and any prepayment model.

Example
Customer pays ₹60K for a 6-month course in January. At Jan-end: ₹10K is revenue, ₹50K is deferred revenue. By June-end, all of it has flowed to revenue.
You'll see it
In every SaaS and subscription-business close.
How to handle
Maintain a deferred revenue schedule per contract, that's how you know exactly how much to recognize each month.
031

Unbilled Revenue work done, invoice still pending

Essentially a flavor of accrued revenue, work performed and revenue earned, but the customer hasn't been invoiced yet (often because the billing cycle hasn't come up, or there's a milestone confirmation pending). Sits as a receivable-type asset on the balance sheet. The distinction from regular AR: AR is invoiced; unbilled is not yet.

Example
Milestone-based project: development complete on 28 Feb but client confirmation expected mid-March. Recognize the milestone revenue at Feb-end as unbilled.
You'll see it
In project-based services businesses, IT services, construction.
How to handle
Track unbilled aging, if work was done months ago and still isn't invoiced, that's a billing process problem, not just an accounting entry.
032

Accounts Receivable (AR) debtors · what customers owe

The total amount owed to the business by customers for invoiced sales. Sits as a current asset on the balance sheet. AR is built up by invoice creation and reduced by customer payments, credit notes, and write-offs. AR balance and AR aging together tell you how much cash is "stuck" with customers and how stale it is.

Example
Total AR on 31 March: ₹3.8Cr across 47 customers. Of that, ₹2.1Cr is within 30 days, ₹1.2Cr 31–60 days, the rest older.
You'll see it
Every collections review, every cash-flow conversation, every audit.
How to handle
Pull the AR aging weekly. Anything 90+ days needs an action, call, email, escalation, or provision.
033

AR Aging how stale is the receivable

A report that bucketing AR by how long each invoice has been outstanding, typically 0–30 days, 31–60, 61–90, 91–180, 180+. The older the bucket, the lower the probability of collection. The aging is the single most important AR management tool you have. Pulled weekly in collection meetings.

Example
"Customer ABC: ₹10L in 0–30, ₹4L in 31–60, ₹2L in 90+. Total ₹16L." That ₹2L 90+ is the worry.
You'll see it
Weekly at minimum, often daily.
How to handle
Track movement, not just snapshots. If the 90+ bucket grew this week, dig into which customer slipped from 60+ to 90+.
034

Bad Debt Provision expected credit loss

An estimated amount of AR that the company expects not to collect, charged as an expense to the P&L and held as a contra-asset against AR. Under Ind AS 109 / IFRS 9, computed using the Expected Credit Loss (ECL) model, based on historical loss patterns, current conditions, and forward-looking information.

Example
Historical data shows 5% of 90+ days AR eventually defaults. Of ₹2Cr in 90+ AR, you provide ₹10L as bad debt expense.
You'll see it
At every period-end, especially year-end.
How to handle
Apply the company's standing ECL policy. Don't invent rates; ask for the documented matrix.
035

Write-off accept the loss, remove from AR

The formal removal of an uncollectible receivable from the books. Differs from provision: a provision anticipates loss while keeping AR on the books; a write-off accepts the loss is real and removes both AR and the provision. Requires management approval at most companies, a write-off is a confession that money is gone.

Example
Customer went bankrupt, recovery effort exhausted, ₹5L written off. Entry: Dr Provision for Bad Debt ₹5L (or Bad Debt Expense if no prior provision), Cr AR ₹5L.
You'll see it
Quarterly or year-end usually.
How to handle
Get written approval before writing off. Document the recovery attempts that justified giving up.
036

Credit Note credit memo · reverse-invoice

A document issued to a customer that reduces the amount they owe, used for returns, billing errors, agreed discounts post-invoice, or service deficiencies. Booked as a reduction in revenue (or as a sales return contra-revenue) and a reduction in AR. In GST terms, a credit note adjusts both the taxable value and the tax already reported.

Example
You invoiced ₹2L plus 18% GST; customer disputed ₹40K of the charge. Issue a credit note for ₹40K + ₹7.2K GST.
You'll see it
Whenever an invoice needs to be partially reversed after booking.
How to handle
Always reference the original invoice on the credit note. Report it in the right GSTR period.
037

Invoice the bill the customer pays from

The legal document raised by the seller asking the buyer to pay. Must contain prescribed information under GST law: GSTIN of both parties, invoice number, date, HSN/SAC code, taxable value, tax components (CGST + SGST or IGST), total. Triggers AR creation and revenue recognition (subject to revenue recognition rules).

Example
"Invoice INV-2025-0428 dated 15 Mar 2026, ₹1,00,000 + ₹18,000 GST = ₹1,18,000 due in 30 days."
You'll see it
Every working day.
How to handle
Invoice errors are expensive to fix downstream. Check GSTIN, HSN, tax rates, and addresses before sending.
038

Tax Invoice GST-compliant invoice

An invoice that meets all the requirements under GST law and entitles the buyer to claim Input Tax Credit (ITC). The legal default for any B2B transaction in India by a GST-registered supplier. Distinguished from a "Bill of Supply" which is used by composition dealers or for exempt supplies, where no GST is charged and no ITC flows.

Example
A registered software vendor issues a Tax Invoice; a small composition-scheme caterer issues a Bill of Supply.
You'll see it
Most B2B invoices you see will be Tax Invoices.
How to handle
Verify it actually qualifies as a Tax Invoice before passing ITC. Missing GSTIN = no ITC = ₹ lost.
039

Proforma Invoice the not-yet-real invoice

A preliminary bill sent to a customer before goods or services are delivered, usually to facilitate advance payment or for internal approval. Has no legal or accounting effect, does not trigger revenue, does not create AR, does not flow into GST returns. Only the final Tax Invoice does.

Example
Customer asks for an estimate for a custom build. You send a proforma ₹50K for budgetary approval. After confirmation and delivery, you raise the actual Tax Invoice.
You'll see it
Often in B2B contexts where advance approval is needed.
How to handle
Never book accounting entries off a proforma. It's not an accounting document.
040

Purchase Order (PO) the buyer's commitment

A formal document issued by the buyer to the seller, authorizing a purchase and committing to pay for it. Includes item, quantity, price, delivery date, payment terms. The PO is the buyer's offer; once accepted by the seller, it becomes a contract. Enterprise vendors typically won't process orders without a PO from the buyer.

Example
A client raises PO #PO-2026-119 for ₹4L worth of services with 30-day payment terms. You quote that PO number on every invoice raised against it.
You'll see it
In every B2B engagement with mid-to-large customers.
How to handle
Never invoice beyond the PO value without a revised PO. Triggers a billing dispute every time.
041

Goods Receipt Note (GRN) proof of delivery

A document confirming that goods (or completion of services) have been received by the buyer, in the agreed quantity and condition. Required before vendor payment is processed in three-way-match systems (PO + Invoice + GRN must all agree). The GRN closes the loop between procurement, delivery, and payment.

Example
Vendor ships 50 laptops. Warehouse counts 50 received in good condition and raises a GRN. Now finance can release payment.
You'll see it
In every payment processing workflow at any client with proper procurement controls.
How to handle
No GRN, no payment release. Don't override this, it's there to prevent paying for things that weren't delivered.
042

Sales Return reverse the sale

When a customer returns goods previously invoiced, the original sale is partially or fully reversed via a credit note. The return reduces revenue (or sits as a contra-revenue called "Sales Returns" if reported separately), reduces AR, and may bring inventory back into stock if applicable. GST on the original invoice is also reversed via the credit note.

Example
Customer returns 10 of 50 laptops as defective. Sales return for ₹5L + ₹90K GST issued; inventory restored.
You'll see it
Frequently in any product-selling business.
How to handle
Ensure the return is recorded in the same period as the credit note. Cross-period returns cause AR vs revenue mismatch.
04

Costs, Expenses & Profitability

Profitability is the layered story of what's left after each round of costs. Gross margin tells you about the unit economics. EBITDA tells you about operating discipline. Net profit tells you what's left for the owners. Each layer has its own diagnostic value, and confusing them is one of the easiest ways to mis-read a business.

043

COGS Cost of Goods Sold · Cost of Revenue

The direct cost of producing the goods or delivering the services that generated the revenue. For a product company: raw materials + direct labor + factory overhead. For a SaaS company: hosting, payment processing fees, customer support, third-party APIs that scale with usage. Subtracting COGS from revenue gives you gross profit.

Example
SaaS revenue ₹15Cr; COGS = AWS ₹1.5Cr + Stripe ₹50L + customer support team ₹1Cr = ₹3Cr. Gross profit ₹12Cr.
You'll see it
On every P&L, prominently.
How to handle
Be precise about what's COGS vs what's OpEx. Putting indirect costs in COGS understates gross margin; the reverse overstates it. Both mislead.
044

OpEx Operating Expense

The ongoing day-to-day cost of running the business that isn't directly tied to delivering the product. Includes salaries (other than direct delivery teams), rent, marketing, sales commissions, software subscriptions for internal use, legal, accounting. OpEx is expensed in the period it's incurred and shows up below gross profit on the P&L.

Example
Office rent of ₹4L/month, monthly Slack subscription of ₹40K, sales team salaries, all OpEx.
You'll see it
Most of your day-to-day expense booking is OpEx.
How to handle
Categorize by department for meaningful management reporting, Sales OpEx, Marketing OpEx, G&A OpEx all tell different stories.
045

CapEx Capital Expenditure

Spending on long-lived assets that will generate value beyond the current period, equipment, machinery, vehicles, owned software, office build-outs. Not expensed when paid; instead, capitalized on the balance sheet as an asset and depreciated/amortized over its useful life. CapEx hits cash flow immediately but the P&L only gradually.

Example
Buying a ₹20L delivery van: paid in full now (cash out), depreciated over 5 years (₹4L/year hits P&L).
You'll see it
Less frequently than OpEx but with larger ticket sizes.
How to handle
Confirm the company's capitalization threshold (typically ₹5K or ₹10K per item). Below that, it's expensed even if technically a long-lived asset.
046

Direct Cost traceable to a product or service

A cost that can be directly attributed to producing a specific unit of output. For a manufacturer: raw materials, direct labor on the assembly line. For a services firm: the consultant's billable hours. Direct costs typically form the bulk of COGS and move proportionally with volume.

Example
The cost of cloth in a t-shirt is a direct cost. So is the embroiderer's wage if they only stitch t-shirts.
You'll see it
In product costing, project costing, and gross margin analysis.
How to handle
When in doubt about whether a cost is direct or indirect, ask: does it disappear if we don't produce this thing? If yes, direct.
047

Indirect Cost overhead

A cost that supports production or delivery but can't be directly attributed to one unit, factory electricity, supervisor salaries, warehouse rent, depreciation on shared equipment. Allocated to products or services using a cost driver (machine hours, labor hours, square footage) for management reporting.

Example
Factory electricity ₹2L/month is indirect, it powers all production lines, so you allocate it based on each line's machine hours.
You'll see it
In cost-sheet preparation and product profitability analysis.
How to handle
Use a consistent allocation method. Switching methods between periods makes period-over-period comparisons meaningless.
048

Fixed Cost stays the same regardless of volume

A cost that doesn't change with the level of business activity, at least within a relevant range. Rent, fixed salaries, depreciation, insurance, fixed-fee software subscriptions. Fixed costs create operating leverage, once revenue covers them, additional revenue mostly flows to profit. They also create risk, when revenue dips, the fixed costs stay.

Example
Office rent ₹5L/month whether you make ₹50L or ₹5Cr in sales that month. Fixed.
You'll see it
In every break-even analysis and budgeting discussion.
How to handle
Identify fixed costs explicitly during budget reviews, they're the floor that revenue must clear before profit begins.
049

Variable Cost scales with volume

A cost that changes in direct proportion to business activity. Raw materials, commissions on sales, transaction processing fees, usage-based hosting costs. Variable costs make the unit economics, gross margin per unit comes from price minus variable cost per unit.

Example
Stripe processing at 2.9% of every transaction is variable, sell more, pay more in fees, proportionally.
You'll see it
In unit economics, contribution margin analysis, scaling discussions.
How to handle
Track variable costs as a percentage of revenue over time, if the percentage drifts up, the unit economics are getting worse.
050

Gross Profit / Gross Margin revenue minus COGS

Gross profit = Revenue - COGS, in rupees. Gross margin = Gross profit ÷ Revenue, in percentage. Tells you the profitability of your core economic activity before any operating overhead. Healthy SaaS gross margins are 70–85%; services 30–50%; products 20–40%; commodity trading 5–10%. Industry-dependent and business-model-dependent.

Example
Revenue ₹15Cr, COGS ₹3Cr. Gross profit ₹12Cr, gross margin 80%. That's a healthy SaaS profile.
You'll see it
On every P&L and in every margin discussion.
How to handle
Track gross margin trend over months, declining margins mean either pricing pressure or cost inflation, both of which need attention.
051

Operating Profit (EBIT) Earnings Before Interest and Tax

Profit from core operations, after both COGS and OpEx, but before interest expense and tax. EBIT measures how profitable the business is at running itself, independent of how it's financed (interest) or where it operates (tax jurisdiction). Useful for comparing operating performance across companies.

Example
Revenue ₹15Cr - COGS ₹3Cr - OpEx ₹9Cr = EBIT ₹3Cr.
You'll see it
On every P&L, in performance comparisons.
How to handle
EBIT and operating profit aren't always identical (some companies exclude non-operating income/expense from EBIT). Read footnotes.
052

EBITDA Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortization

EBIT plus depreciation and amortization. Strips out non-cash charges and financing structure, giving a rough proxy for operating cash generation. Investors love it; auditors are cautious about it. EBITDA is not a GAAP/Ind AS metric, companies define it slightly differently, so always check the definition before comparing.

Example
EBIT ₹3Cr + Depreciation ₹40L + Amortization ₹20L = EBITDA ₹3.6Cr.
You'll see it
In every investor deck, every valuation discussion, every leverage covenant.
How to handle
Never use EBITDA as a substitute for cash flow, it ignores working capital, CapEx, and taxes, all of which consume real cash.
053

Net Profit PAT · bottom line

Profit After Tax, what's left after deducting every expense including COGS, OpEx, depreciation, amortization, interest, and income tax. Flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet. The final accounting measure of profitability for the period.

Example
EBIT ₹3Cr - Interest ₹20L - Tax ₹70L = PAT ₹2.1Cr.
You'll see it
The number everyone quotes when they say "we made X this year."
How to handle
PAT can be distorted by one-off items (asset sale gains, exceptional write-offs). Always look at PAT alongside operating profit to see the underlying trend.
054

Depreciation spreading tangible asset cost

The systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible fixed asset (laptops, machinery, vehicles, buildings) over its useful life. Booked as an expense on the P&L and as accumulated depreciation (contra-asset) on the balance sheet. The Income Tax Act prescribes specific rates for tax depreciation; the Companies Act allows useful-life-based methods (typically straight-line) for book depreciation.

Example
A ₹60K laptop with a 3-year useful life depreciates at ₹20K/year (straight-line), ₹1,667/month.
You'll see it
Every month-end, as a recurring journal entry.
How to handle
Maintain a fixed asset register. The monthly depreciation entry should be generated from the register, not calculated from scratch each time.
055

Amortization depreciation's intangible cousin

The systematic allocation of the cost of an intangible asset (software licenses owned, patents, goodwill, capitalized R&D) over its useful life. Mechanically identical to depreciation, just for intangibles. Goodwill is not amortized under Ind AS but tested annually for impairment.

Example
A ₹6L perpetual software license amortized over 5 years = ₹1.2L/year of amortization expense.
You'll see it
When the client has owned software or acquired intangible assets.
How to handle
Confirm useful life, for purchased software, often 3–5 years. Document the basis.
056

Impairment writing down asset value

When the carrying value of an asset on the balance sheet exceeds its recoverable amount (what it can realistically generate going forward), the difference is recognized as an impairment loss. Applies to fixed assets, intangibles, investments, and goodwill. Triggered by events like obsolescence, market downturns, loss of key customers, regulatory changes.

Example
A ₹50L specialized machine becomes obsolete when the customer who used 80% of its output exits. Impair to ₹15L recoverable value; book ₹35L impairment loss.
You'll see it
In annual reviews and when material business changes occur.
How to handle
Impairment is a senior call, not a clerical one. Flag possible impairment triggers; let leadership decide on amount.
057

Provision recognizing a probable future liability

A liability of uncertain timing or amount, recognized when there's a present obligation, an outflow is probable, and the amount can be reliably estimated. Common provisions: bad debt provision, warranty provision, provision for taxation, provision for gratuity. Hits the P&L as an expense, sits on the balance sheet as a liability until utilized or reversed.

Example
A pending tax dispute with high probability of ruling against the company: provide ₹40L for the expected outflow.
You'll see it
At year-end and in any quarterly close where new obligations crystallize.
How to handle
Don't confuse provisions (liabilities) with reserves (equity). They look similar in name; they're fundamentally different things.
05

Payables, Cash & Working Capital

The other side of AR is AP, what you owe vendors. Bank reconciliation is the daily reality check that the books match the bank. Working capital metrics (DSO, DPO, DIO) tell you whether the business is running tight or loose, and where cash is getting trapped or freed up.

Diagram 05: The Cash Conversion Cycle
How long, on average, the business waits between paying out cash for inputs and collecting cash from customers. Lower is better. Negative is exceptional (you collect before you pay: Amazon, Apple, and most marketplaces do this).
058

Accounts Payable (AP) creditors · what we owe vendors

The total amount owed to vendors for invoices received but not yet paid. Sits as a current liability on the balance sheet. Built up by vendor invoice booking and reduced by payments and debit notes. Managing AP well means paying on time (preserving vendor relationships) but not earlier than needed (preserving cash).

Example
Total AP on 31 March: ₹2.2Cr across 80 vendors. Of that, ₹1.5Cr is within payment terms; ₹70L is overdue.
You'll see it
Every payment run, every cash-flow review.
How to handle
Maintain payment-terms discipline. Paying early gives away cash; paying late damages relationships and incurs late fees or interest.
059

AP Aging how overdue is the payable

A report bucketing AP by how long each vendor invoice has been outstanding past its payment due date, typically 0–30, 31–60, 61–90, 90+ days. High aging signals cash pressure or process breakdown in invoice approval. Aging reports are pulled before every payment cycle to decide which invoices to pay first.

Example
₹50L in 0–30 (within terms), ₹30L in 31–60 (slightly late), ₹20L in 90+ (vendor will be calling).
You'll see it
Weekly in any disciplined finance function.
How to handle
Pay 90+ first to avoid escalation and reputation damage. Then 60+, then within-terms in priority order.
060

Bank Reconciliation BRS · matching books to bank

The process of matching the company's cash book or bank GL to the actual bank statement, identifying and explaining every difference. Differences arise from timing (cheques not yet cleared, deposits in transit) and errors (entries in one but not the other). Done daily or weekly, never less than monthly.

Example
Bank balance ₹52L; book balance ₹50L. Difference: a ₹2L cheque issued but not yet cleared. Reconciliation explains it.
You'll see it
Every reporting cycle.
How to handle
Never close month-end with unexplained reconciling items. Each variance must be identified and either cleared or carried forward with explanation.
061

Petty Cash small-amount cash on hand

A small cash float kept in the office for minor expenses too small or urgent to process through a vendor payment, courier fees, taxi reimbursements, office supplies. Managed via an imprest system: fixed float, topped up periodically based on actual spend. Vouchers are required for every disbursement; surprise audits keep it honest.

Example
₹10K float in the office; ₹6K spent on chai, courier, autos in two weeks; reimburse ₹6K to bring the float back to ₹10K.
You'll see it
In any company that has a physical office and small operating outflows.
How to handle
Strict voucher discipline. Surprise count monthly. Petty cash is also where small leakages live undetected.
062

Working Capital current assets minus current liabilities

The capital tied up in day-to-day operations. Working capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities. Positive working capital means the company can fund short-term obligations from short-term assets. Negative working capital can be a red flag (struggling to pay vendors) or a feature (some business models like quick-commerce run on it deliberately).

Example
CA ₹6Cr (Cash 1, AR 3, Inventory 2) - CL ₹4Cr (AP 2.5, taxes 0.5, accruals 1) = Working Capital ₹2Cr.
You'll see it
In every cash position review and balance sheet analysis.
How to handle
Watch the trend, not just the absolute number. Shrinking working capital with growing revenue can mean efficiency or strain, context decides.
063

DSO Days Sales Outstanding

Average number of days the company takes to collect cash from customers after a sale. Calculated as (AR / Revenue) × number of days in the period. Lower DSO = faster cash collection = better. Tracking DSO trend reveals collection process health. Benchmark by industry: SaaS subscription on cards is sub-7 days; B2B services 45–60 days; enterprise sales 90+ days isn't unusual.

Example
AR ₹3Cr, Quarterly revenue ₹9Cr → DSO = (3/9) × 90 = 30 days.
You'll see it
In every cash management dashboard.
How to handle
Use rolling 3-month revenue rather than spot-period revenue for stability. Discuss DSO movement in weekly collections meetings.
064

DPO Days Payable Outstanding

Average number of days the company takes to pay vendors after receiving an invoice. Calculated as (AP / COGS or Purchases) × days. Higher DPO = the company is holding onto its cash longer = improved working capital, but at the cost of vendor relationships if pushed too far. Healthy DPO is typically aligned with negotiated terms.

Example
AP ₹2Cr, Quarterly purchases ₹6Cr → DPO = (2/6) × 90 = 30 days.
You'll see it
In treasury reviews and working capital optimization discussions.
How to handle
Compare DPO to the average negotiated terms. DPO of 60 days against 30-day terms means systemic late payment.
065

DIO Days Inventory Outstanding

Average number of days inventory sits before being sold. Calculated as (Inventory / COGS) × days. Lower DIO = faster inventory turnover = less working capital tied up in stock. Only relevant for product businesses; SaaS and most services have zero inventory.

Example
Inventory ₹4Cr, Quarterly COGS ₹12Cr → DIO = (4/12) × 90 = 30 days.
You'll see it
In retail, manufacturing, distribution clients.
How to handle
Watch by SKU. Aggregate DIO can hide that fast-moving items turn in 10 days while slow-movers sit for 200.
066

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) DIO + DSO − DPO

The total time, in days, between when the company pays for inputs and when it collects cash from customers. CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO. The lower (or more negative), the better, the business funds itself rather than tying up capital. A growing CCC means the business is increasingly cash-hungry as it scales.

Example
DIO 30 + DSO 45 - DPO 30 = CCC 45 days of cash tied up.
You'll see it
In any serious working capital analysis.
How to handle
Lever individually, pushing DPO too hard damages vendors; pushing DSO too hard damages customers; reducing DIO requires demand forecasting. Pick the easiest lever.
067

Float cash in transit between book and bank

The amount of money that has been initiated for transfer but hasn't yet settled in the destination account. Includes outstanding cheques (book shows paid, bank doesn't), deposits in transit (book shows received, bank doesn't yet). Float is what creates timing differences in bank reconciliation. Goes to zero with instant payment systems like UPI / IMPS.

Example
Cheque of ₹3L issued to a vendor on Friday; cleared by bank on Tuesday. The ₹3L is float over the weekend.
You'll see it
Especially in cheque-heavy industries and cross-border transactions.
How to handle
Don't promise vendors instant clearing for cheques. Use NEFT/RTGS for predictable timing.
068

Bank Statement the bank's record of your account

The bank's record of all credits, debits, and balances on your account for a period. The source of truth for cash position (assuming the bank hasn't made errors, they occasionally do). Reconciled monthly against the cash book GL. Available in downloadable form (PDF, MT940, CSV, OFX) from net banking.

Example
April HDFC statement: opening ₹50L, total credits ₹4Cr, total debits ₹3.8Cr, closing ₹70L.
You'll see it
Every reconciliation routine.
How to handle
Download in machine-readable format (MT940/OFX) if your accounting system supports auto-reconciliation. Saves hours.
069

BRS Bank Reconciliation Statement

The output of the bank reconciliation process, a statement listing all unreconciled items (cheques issued but uncleared, deposits in transit, bank charges not yet booked, errors) that explain the gap between book and bank balance. Required as audit evidence. Should be prepared and signed off monthly.

Example
"Book ₹50L + Cheques issued not cleared ₹3L - Deposits in transit ₹1L = Bank ₹52L. Reconciled."
You'll see it
Monthly at every client.
How to handle
Persistent items that don't clear after 60 days probably indicate a real error or an issued-but-lost cheque. Investigate, don't keep carrying.
06

Indian Tax & Compliance

India's tax system is dense, deadline-driven, and unforgiving of mistakes. GST monthly, TDS monthly, advance tax quarterly, ITR annually, audit annually, and each has its own forms, due dates, and penalty regime. Get the vocabulary right and the calendar follows; get the calendar right and the rest is execution.

Diagram 06: How GST flows through a supply chain
GST is a value-added tax. At each step, the seller charges output GST on the sale, but claims input GST credit on the purchase. Only the value added at each step is effectively taxed. The final consumer bears the full tax.
When a vendor doesn't file their GSTR-1, their GST shows up in your GSTR-2B with delays or gaps. Your ITC claim depends on their compliance, which is why vendor follow-up is part of the finance function, not just procurement's.
070

GST Goods and Services Tax

India's unified indirect tax on the supply of goods and services. Replaced multiple cascading taxes (VAT, service tax, excise) with a single value-added tax. Comes in three forms based on transaction nature: CGST (Central) + SGST (State) for intra-state sales; IGST (Integrated) for inter-state sales and imports. Common rates: 0%, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%. Most B2B services attract 18%.

Example
Karnataka business selling to Karnataka customer: 9% CGST + 9% SGST. Same selling to Maharashtra customer: 18% IGST.
You'll see it
On every invoice, every payment, every return.
How to handle
Determine place of supply correctly, that decides CGST/SGST vs IGST. Wrong tax type creates ITC mismatch issues for the customer.
071

GSTIN GST Identification Number

A 15-character alphanumeric registration number issued to every GST-registered taxpayer. State code (2 digits) + PAN (10) + entity number (1) + Z (1) + check digit (1). Required on every tax invoice. Verifying customer GSTIN before invoicing is a basic discipline, wrong GSTIN means the customer can't claim ITC.

Example
GSTIN 29AABCT1234R1Z5 reads: 29 (Karnataka) + AABCT1234R (PAN) + 1 (first entity) + Z + 5 (check).
You'll see it
On every B2B invoice.
How to handle
Verify GSTIN on the GST portal before raising an invoice to a new customer. Takes 30 seconds; saves hours of correction.
072

GSTR-1 monthly return of outward supplies

The monthly (or quarterly for small taxpayers) return where the seller reports every sale invoice raised, B2B, B2C, exports, credit notes, debit notes. Due on the 11th of the following month for monthly filers. The data filed in GSTR-1 by a seller is what eventually shows up in the buyer's GSTR-2A/2B. Errors propagate to customer ITC.

Example
March GSTR-1 due by 11 April: report every B2B invoice with buyer GSTIN, invoice number, taxable value, tax components.
You'll see it
Monthly. The first deadline of every month's GST cycle.
How to handle
Reconcile sales register to GSTR-1 before filing. Filed errors are amendable but flagged, better to file correctly the first time.
073

GSTR-3B monthly summary return and payment

The monthly summary return where the taxpayer declares total outward supplies, total ITC claimed, and pays the net GST liability. Due on the 20th of the following month for monthly filers. GSTR-3B is the return that triggers actual tax payment; not filing it incurs late fees and interest.

Example
March GSTR-3B due 20 April: declare ₹50L taxable sales, ₹9L output GST, ₹6L ITC claimed, net payable ₹3L. Pay and file.
You'll see it
Monthly. The second deadline of every month's GST cycle.
How to handle
Make sure ITC claimed in 3B matches available ITC in GSTR-2B. Over-claiming triggers interest and notice.
074

GSTR-2A / 2B auto-populated inward supplies

Auto-generated statements showing all inward supplies (purchases) made by you, based on what your vendors filed in their GSTR-1. GSTR-2A is dynamic, keeps updating as vendors file. GSTR-2B is static, finalized on the 14th of each month and used as the basis for ITC claim. Reconciling your purchase register against 2B is monthly mandatory hygiene.

Example
You booked a vendor invoice with ₹18K GST in March; GSTR-2B for March shows ₹18K from that vendor. Match. Claim ITC.
You'll see it
Monthly, around the 14th.
How to handle
For mismatches, vendor not yet filed, wrong tax rate, wrong invoice value, follow up with the vendor before filing 3B. Otherwise, ITC is lost or deferred.
075

Input Tax Credit (ITC) offset for GST paid on purchases

The GST you paid on purchases (input) can be set off against the GST you collected on sales (output). You only pay the net difference to the government. Available only when the vendor has filed their GSTR-1 (i.e., the invoice appears in your GSTR-2B). Some categories of expense don't qualify (e.g., personal use, blocked credits under Section 17(5)).

Example
Output GST on sales ₹10L; ITC on purchases ₹6L; net payment to government ₹4L.
You'll see it
In every GST workings and reconciliation.
How to handle
Don't claim ITC that's not in 2B. Don't claim ITC on blocked credit categories. Keep documentary evidence, auditors check.
076

Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) recipient pays the GST

In normal GST, the supplier collects and pays the tax. Under reverse charge, the recipient pays the GST directly to the government instead. Applicable on specific notified services (legal services from advocates, GTA transport, services from unregistered suppliers in certain cases). The recipient can claim ITC on the GST so paid.

Example
Lawyer issues a bill for ₹50K (no GST on the invoice). You pay ₹9K GST to the government under RCM and book it as expense + ITC.
You'll see it
When dealing with advocates, GTAs, and certain unregistered service providers.
How to handle
Identify RCM transactions when booking, they need a separate JE for the GST liability. Easy to miss; easy to get a notice for.
077

TDS Tax Deducted at Source

Income tax that the payer deducts from a payment before passing the balance to the recipient, then deposits with the government on the recipient's behalf. Applies to specific payment categories: salary, professional fees, contractor payments, rent, interest, commission. Each category has its own section of the Income Tax Act and its own rate.

Example
Consultancy fee of ₹1L: TDS 10% under Section 194J = ₹10K deducted; pay vendor ₹90K; deposit ₹10K to govt by 7th of next month.
You'll see it
On every professional service payment, contractor payment, and salary payment.
How to handle
Know the major TDS sections (192 salary, 194C contractor, 194J professional, 194I rent). Deposit by 7th, file return by 31st of month following the quarter.
078

TCS Tax Collected at Source

Opposite of TDS, instead of the payer deducting tax, the seller collects extra tax over and above the sale price and deposits it on the buyer's behalf. Applies to specific transaction types (scrap sales, motor vehicle above ₹10L, foreign remittances under LRS above ₹7L, e-commerce sales). Buyer can claim the TCS against their tax liability.

Example
Foreign tour package of ₹5L: seller adds 5% TCS = ₹25K. Buyer pays ₹5.25L; ₹25K goes to govt against the buyer's PAN.
You'll see it
In specific transaction types only, not as widespread as TDS.
How to handle
Verify applicability before collecting. Collecting TCS where not required creates customer disputes.
079

Form 26AS consolidated tax statement

A consolidated statement maintained by the Income Tax Department for every PAN holder, showing all TDS deducted, TCS collected, advance tax paid, self-assessment tax paid, refunds, and high-value transactions reported by banks and registrars. The reference for any ITR filing, what's in your Form 26AS is what the department knows.

Example
Before filing ITR, download Form 26AS and reconcile to ensure all TDS deducted by your customers actually shows up.
You'll see it
At ITR filing time, and whenever investigating a TDS dispute.
How to handle
If TDS deducted by a customer isn't in 26AS, they haven't filed their TDS return. Chase them; otherwise you can't claim the credit.
080

Form 16 annual TDS certificate for salary

The annual TDS certificate issued by an employer to a salaried employee, summarizing total salary paid, deductions claimed, and total TDS deducted during the financial year. Required for the employee to file their personal ITR. Must be issued by 15 June following the FY-end. Has two parts: Part A (TDS deposit summary), Part B (salary computation).

Example
Employee's Form 16 for FY26: gross salary ₹15L, 80C deduction ₹1.5L, taxable ₹13.5L, tax ₹1.2L (all deducted as TDS through the year).
You'll see it
Every June, as part of post-FY-end payroll closure.
How to handle
Generate from the payroll system after FY-end. Cross-check Part A against the TDS return filed (Form 24Q), discrepancies need fixing before issue.
081

Form 16A quarterly TDS certificate for non-salary

The quarterly TDS certificate issued for non-salary payments where TDS was deducted, professional fees, contractor payments, rent, interest. Different from Form 16 (which is for salary). The deductee uses Form 16A to claim TDS credit in their ITR.

Example
You deducted ₹10K TDS on a contractor's invoice in April. Issue Form 16A for Q1 by 15 August.
You'll see it
Every quarter, generated from the TRACES portal.
How to handle
Generate and send to vendors proactively. Vendors will chase you for them otherwise.
082

PAN Permanent Account Number

A 10-character alphanumeric identifier issued by the Income Tax Department to every taxpayer, individual or entity. PAN is the universal tax identifier; every transaction, return, deduction, and payment is linked to it. The first 5 characters are letters, next 4 are digits, last is a letter (check digit).

Example
Vendor's PAN: AABCT1234R. Used in every TDS workings, every Form 26AS, every ITR.
You'll see it
On every TDS-related transaction.
How to handle
If a vendor doesn't provide a valid PAN, TDS rate doubles or 20%, whichever is higher (Section 206AA). Get PANs upfront in vendor onboarding.
083

TAN Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number

A 10-character alphanumeric identifier issued to any entity that deducts TDS or collects TCS. Required to deposit TDS with the government and file TDS returns. Separate from PAN, every business that has employees or makes deductible payments must have a TAN.

Example
Employer's TAN: BLRX12345E. Mentioned on every TDS challan, every TDS return, every Form 16/16A.
You'll see it
On every TDS-related document.
How to handle
One TAN per location is the norm; large entities may have multiple. Use the correct TAN for the relevant deduction location.
084

HSN Code Harmonized System of Nomenclature

A 4 to 8-digit code classifying goods for GST purposes. Globally standardized at the 6-digit level; India extends to 8 digits. Required on tax invoices, 4 digits if turnover < ₹5Cr, 6 digits if turnover ≥ ₹5Cr. Determines the applicable GST rate. Mis-coding can lead to wrong tax rate and disputes.

Example
Laptop sales: HSN 8471 at 18% GST.
You'll see it
On every goods invoice.
How to handle
Maintain an HSN master per SKU. Don't let invoice creators improvise codes, they'll guess wrong.
085

SAC Code Service Accounting Code

The services equivalent of HSN, a 6-digit code classifying services for GST. Required on every service invoice. Most B2B services fall under SAC 9983 (other professional services), 998314 (IT consultancy), 998361 (advertising). Each has a defined GST rate.

Example
Software development service: SAC 998313 at 18% GST.
You'll see it
On every service invoice.
How to handle
Map each service line to a SAC during product setup. Reuse, don't re-invent per invoice.
086

e-Invoicing government-authenticated invoice

Mandatory for businesses with turnover above the prescribed threshold (currently ₹5Cr): every B2B invoice must be uploaded to the GST portal in real time, which returns a unique Invoice Reference Number (IRN) and QR code that must be printed on the invoice. Without IRN, the invoice is not legally valid.

Example
You raise a B2B invoice for ₹2L. Upload to e-invoice portal, receive IRN within seconds, print IRN and QR on the invoice, send to customer.
You'll see it
In every applicable B2B billing workflow.
How to handle
Integrate the billing system with the IRP (Invoice Registration Portal) via APIs. Manual upload doesn't scale.
087

e-Way Bill goods movement permit

An electronic permit required for the movement of goods of value exceeding ₹50K within India. Generated on the e-Way Bill portal before dispatch; contains supplier, recipient, vehicle, and consignment details. Validity is based on distance (one day per 200km). Goods stopped without a valid e-Way Bill can be detained and penalized.

Example
Dispatching laptops worth ₹4L from Bengaluru to Mumbai: generate e-Way Bill, valid for ~5 days.
You'll see it
In product / logistics businesses.
How to handle
Generate before goods leave the warehouse, not after. Late generation invites notice.
07

Payroll Components

A salary is not a single number. It's a stack of components, each with its own tax treatment, its own statutory rule, and its own line on the payslip. Understanding the stack is what lets you answer the most common question employees ask finance: "why is my CTC ₹15L but my bank credit ₹85K?"

088

CTC Cost to Company

The total annual cost the company incurs for an employee, includes everything: base pay, allowances, employer PF contribution, employer ESIC, gratuity provision, insurance premiums, bonuses, ESOPs (often quoted at face value). CTC is what's in the offer letter. It is not what the employee receives in hand. The gap between CTC and in-hand can easily be 25–35%.

Example
CTC ₹15L: gross salary ₹13.5L + employer PF ₹70K + gratuity ₹50K + insurance ₹30K = ₹15L.
You'll see it
In every offer letter, every appraisal letter, every cost analysis.
How to handle
When discussing compensation with anyone, clarify whether you mean CTC, gross, or net. Three different numbers, all called "salary" casually.
089

Gross Salary salary before deductions

The salary payable to the employee before any deductions, basic + HRA + special allowance + LTA + medical + any other allowances + variable components paid in the month. Gross excludes employer-side contributions (employer PF, gratuity, insurance), those are part of CTC but not gross. Gross is what hits the payroll register before TDS and PF.

Example
Monthly gross ₹1.12L = Basic ₹45K + HRA ₹22.5K + Special Allowance ₹40K + Medical ₹4.5K.
You'll see it
In every payroll run.
How to handle
Gross is your accrual base for salary expense, book gross to P&L, then split deductions to the right payable accounts.
090

Net Salary Take-home · what hits the bank

Gross salary minus all employee-side deductions, employee PF (12% of basic), professional tax, TDS on salary, any loan EMIs, voluntary deductions. The amount actually credited to the employee's bank account. The number the employee actually cares about.

Example
Gross ₹1.12L - PF ₹5.4K - PT ₹200 - TDS ₹12K = Net ₹94.4K credited to bank.
You'll see it
In every payroll register and bank transfer file.
How to handle
Most "my salary is wrong" employee tickets are confusion between gross and net. Show them the deductions one by one.
091

Basic Salary the foundation component

The core salary component on which most other components and statutory deductions are calculated. Typically 40–50% of CTC. Higher basic means higher PF, higher gratuity, higher leave encashment, better long-term benefits but lower in-hand. Lower basic does the opposite. There's no statutory minimum percentage; companies decide based on what to optimize for.

Example
CTC ₹15L; Basic at 45% = ₹6.75L annual = ₹56.25K/month.
You'll see it
On every payslip, prominently.
How to handle
If a company changes basic structure mid-year, employee net pay shifts. Communicate clearly before processing.
092

HRA House Rent Allowance

An allowance paid to employees to cover rent. Partially tax-exempt under Section 10(13A) if the employee actually pays rent. Exemption is the least of: actual HRA received, 50% of basic (metro) or 40% (non-metro), or rent paid minus 10% of basic. Employees claim it by submitting rent receipts and (for rent above ₹1L/year) the landlord's PAN.

Example
Basic ₹50K, HRA ₹25K, rent paid ₹30K in Bengaluru: exempt = min(25K, 25K, 30K-5K) = ₹25K. Fully exempt.
You'll see it
In TDS computation and at investment-declaration time.
How to handle
Get rent receipts at the start of the year and revisit at year-end. Wrong HRA exemption = wrong TDS = year-end correction or refund.
093

EPF / PF Employees' Provident Fund

A statutory retirement savings scheme. Employee contributes 12% of basic + DA; employer matches 12% (8.33% goes to EPS, pension fund, capped on ₹15K basic, balance to EPF). Mandatory for employees earning basic + DA ≤ ₹15K/month; optional above. Withdrawn at retirement, resignation (after 2-month gap), or for specified purposes (housing, medical).

Example
Basic ₹50K: employee PF ₹6K, employer PF ₹6K (₹1,250 to EPS, ₹4,750 to EPF). Total monthly inflow to EPF ₹10,750.
You'll see it
On every payslip, in every PF challan filed by the 15th of next month.
How to handle
Don't miss the 15th deadline. Late deposit = interest + penalty + employee complaints.
094

ESIC Employee State Insurance Corporation

A statutory health insurance scheme for lower-income employees. Employee contributes 0.75% of gross wages, employer contributes 3.25%. Mandatory for employees earning gross ≤ ₹21K/month (₹25K for persons with disability) and applicable when the establishment has 10 or more such employees. Contribution funds medical care, sickness benefit, maternity benefit.

Example
Office helper earning ₹18K: ESIC employee ₹135, employer ₹585. Total ₹720/month to ESIC.
You'll see it
In payroll for clients with support/admin staff in lower wage brackets.
How to handle
Deposit by 15th of next month. Wages excluded from ESIC are limited, when in doubt, include and check.
095

Professional Tax (PT) state-level employment tax

A small state-government tax on employment income, levied by some Indian states (Karnataka, Maharashtra, West Bengal, Telangana and others). Rates vary by state and salary slab; typically capped at ₹2,500 per year per employee. Deducted by employer monthly from employee salary and deposited to the state.

Example
Karnataka employee earning above ₹15K: ₹200/month PT deducted. Annual ₹2,400.
You'll see it
On every payslip in PT-applicable states.
How to handle
Know which states your employees work in, PT applies based on work location, not company HQ. Each state has its own portal and rules.
096

Gratuity long-service lump sum

A statutory lump sum payable to employees who complete 5 or more years of continuous service, calculated as (last drawn basic + DA) × 15/26 × years of service. Funded either through provisioning on the books or via a gratuity trust / LIC group gratuity policy. Hits the P&L as an annual provision based on actuarial valuation for larger companies.

Example
Employee with last basic ₹80K, 8 years of service: gratuity = 80,000 × (15/26) × 8 = ₹3.69L on exit.
You'll see it
In annual provisioning and at exit settlements.
How to handle
Tax-free up to ₹20L for non-government employees. Above that, taxable. Verify exemption status before computing TDS at exit.
097

New vs Old Tax Regime two parallel income tax structures

Since FY 2020-21, Indian salaried taxpayers can choose between the old regime (lower slab limits, but ~70+ deductions and exemptions available, HRA, 80C, 80D, home loan interest, etc.) and the new regime (higher slab limits, lower rates, but most deductions removed). The new regime is now the default; employees must opt for old explicitly at the start of the FY. Choice affects monthly TDS.

Example
Employee earning ₹15L: Old regime tax ~₹1.5L with full deductions; new regime ~₹1.4L without any. Math depends on what deductions are available.
You'll see it
At every employee onboarding and FY-start declaration cycle.
How to handle
Collect regime preference declarations in April. Switching mid-year is allowed but messy, encourage early decision.
08

Audit, Controls & MIS

Audits are the external check on whether your books reflect reality. Internal controls are the day-to-day system that prevents errors and fraud from happening in the first place. MIS reports are the management layer above the books, turning raw numbers into decisions. All three exist to answer the same question: do we trust our own data enough to act on it?

098

Statutory Audit the Companies Act audit

Mandatory annual audit of a company's financial statements by an independent chartered accountant, under the Companies Act 2013. The auditor expresses an opinion on whether the financial statements give a "true and fair view" of the company's affairs. Required for every company regardless of size or turnover. Output is the audit report, attached to filed financials.

Example
FY26 statutory audit happens between April and September 2026; signed audit report dated say 30 August.
You'll see it
Every year, April through September.
How to handle
Maintain audit-ready records year-round, not at the last minute. Auditors will ask for samples of every major process, be ready.
099

Tax Audit Section 44AB audit

Mandatory audit under Income Tax Act Section 44AB for businesses with turnover above ₹1Cr (₹10Cr if cash transactions are below 5%) and professionals above ₹50L. Conducted by a CA, output is Form 3CA/3CB + 3CD, a detailed disclosure of tax positions taken. Due by 30 September of the following FY. Penalty for non-filing is 0.5% of turnover or ₹1.5L, whichever lower.

Example
Company with ₹15Cr turnover: tax audit mandatory; Form 3CD filed by 30 Sep along with ITR.
You'll see it
Annually, around August-September.
How to handle
The 3CD asks ~50 disclosures. Many require year-long documentation (loans, related parties, depreciation, payments above thresholds). Maintain working files all year.
100

Internal Audit the operational check

A periodic review of operational and financial processes by an internal team or outsourced firm, focused on control effectiveness, process efficiency, and risk management. Mandatory under the Companies Act for listed companies and certain unlisted thresholds (turnover ≥ ₹200Cr or borrowings ≥ ₹100Cr). Outputs are observations and recommendations, not opinions on financials.

Example
Internal audit of the procurement process: tests 30 random POs to check PO-Invoice-GRN matching, vendor due diligence, approval limits.
You'll see it
Quarterly or half-yearly in larger clients.
How to handle
Treat internal audit observations as a chance to fix systems before the statutory auditor finds them, same issues, less pressure.
101

Internal Controls the prevention layer

The policies and procedures designed to ensure reliable financial reporting, safeguard assets, and prevent fraud. Examples: dual authorization for payments above a threshold, monthly bank reconciliation sign-off, approval workflows for vendor onboarding, segregation of duties (the person who creates a vendor cannot pay it). Strong internal controls reduce errors and make audit cleaner.

Example
"Payments above ₹5L require two-person approval; vendor master changes require finance head sign-off." Both internal controls.
You'll see it
Embedded in every workflow you operate within.
How to handle
Don't shortcut internal controls "just this once." Each shortcut creates audit risk and operational risk.
102

Audit Trail who did what when

A chronological record of every transaction and every change to that transaction, including who created it, who edited it, when, and what was the old vs new value. Mandatory in accounting software for companies since 1 April 2023 under MCA notification, the system must capture this and cannot allow it to be disabled. Reviewed by auditors as part of the IT general controls assessment.

Example
An expense entry was created by user A on Mon, edited by user B on Wed to change the amount from ₹5K to ₹50K. Both events are in the audit trail.
You'll see it
In every accounting system change, every audit review.
How to handle
Never use shared logins or generic "admin" accounts, audit trail loses meaning. Each person needs a named user.
103

MIS Report Management Information System

A reporting package prepared periodically (typically monthly) for management decision-making, usually includes P&L (actual vs budget vs prior period), key operational metrics, cash position, AR/AP aging, headcount and productivity. The MIS is what gets reviewed in management meetings; structured for decision support, not statutory compliance.

Example
March MIS: revenue ₹1.4Cr (vs budget ₹1.5Cr, -7%), gross margin 78% (vs 80% last month), cash ₹6.5Cr (runway 8 months).
You'll see it
Every month-end, by the 10th of the following month.
How to handle
MIS quality depends on accurate close. Sacrificing close accuracy to publish MIS faster is a false economy, wrong numbers are worse than late ones.
104

Variance Analysis actual vs plan, explained

The exercise of comparing actual results against budget or prior period and explaining each material difference. A variance is a number; an explained variance is information. Volume variance, price variance, mix variance, timing variance, naming the driver is what makes the analysis useful for next-period decisions.

Example
"Revenue ₹1.4Cr vs budget ₹1.5Cr. ₹10L shortfall driven by 2 large deals slipping to April. Underlying run-rate is on track."
You'll see it
In every monthly review meeting.
How to handle
If you can't explain a material variance, that's where the next investigation goes. Unexplained variances ≠ noise; they're hidden information.
105

Budget vs Forecast two different tools, often confused

A budget is the annual plan locked at the start of the year, a target. A forecast is the periodic update of where you actually expect to land, a prediction based on current run-rate. Budget doesn't change after lock; forecast updates monthly or quarterly. Comparing actuals to both tells you whether you're missing the original plan and whether your current trajectory is recovering or worsening.

Example
FY26 Budget: revenue ₹18Cr. Q2 forecast: ₹17Cr (slipped). Actual H1: ₹8.2Cr. Both reference points are needed.
You'll see it
In every financial planning cycle.
How to handle
Don't let the budget become a "forecast" by re-baselining it mid-year. The whole point of the budget is that it doesn't move.
106

Maker-Checker two-person workflow control

A control mechanism where one person ("maker") initiates or records a transaction and another ("checker") reviews and approves it before it takes effect. Used heavily in payments, journal entries above thresholds, master data changes (vendor adds, customer updates). Prevents both errors and unilateral fraud, neither person can complete the transaction alone.

Example
Junior accountant creates a vendor payment of ₹3L; senior reviews invoice, approval, and bank account before releasing. Both actions are logged.
You'll see it
In every payment platform and most accounting systems.
How to handle
Never bypass the checker step, even for urgent items. Urgency is the most common excuse for fraud.
107

Segregation of Duties SoD · no single person owns everything

The principle that incompatible duties, recording transactions, authorizing transactions, and custody of assets, should be assigned to different people. The person who books vendor invoices should not be the person who approves vendor payments. The person who maintains the inventory record should not be the person who handles physical inventory. Reduces both error and fraud opportunity.

Example
Accountant A: books invoices. Accountant B: approves payments. Treasury manager: releases bank transfer. Three separate hands; harder to game.
You'll see it
In role design and access control reviews.
How to handle
In small finance teams, perfect SoD isn't possible, compensate with compensating controls (regular review by an independent person, surprise audits).
09

SaaS & Tech Finance Metrics

A SaaS business is read through metrics the traditional P&L doesn't show on its face: recurring revenue, retention quality, customer acquisition economics, cash runway. These are the metrics investors track and management runs the business by. If you're at a tech client, these matter more than the P&L on most days.

108

MRR Monthly Recurring Revenue

The total predictable subscription revenue normalized to a monthly figure. Calculated as the sum of monthly fees from all active subscriptions; annual plans divided by 12. Excludes one-time setup fees, professional services, and non-recurring usage charges. MRR is the heartbeat metric of a subscription business.

Example
120 customers paying ₹50K/month average = MRR ₹60L. Annual plans worth ₹12L/year contribute ₹1L/month each.
You'll see it
In every SaaS dashboard, every investor update.
How to handle
Track MRR movement components: new MRR + expansion MRR + reactivation MRR - contraction MRR - churn MRR = net new MRR.
109

ARR Annual Recurring Revenue

The annualized version of MRR, typically MRR × 12. Represents the run-rate annual recurring revenue if all current subscriptions continued at current values for 12 months. ARR is the headline number for SaaS valuation. Often a different number from actual revenue earned in the trailing 12 months, especially if growth is fast.

Example
MRR ₹60L → ARR ₹7.2Cr. If you grew from ₹40L to ₹60L MRR over the year, actual revenue earned might be ₹6Cr, not ₹7.2Cr.
You'll see it
In every fundraising conversation and SaaS investor update.
How to handle
ARR is the forward-looking number; revenue is the historical one. Use both; never substitute one for the other.
110

NRR Net Revenue Retention

Of the revenue you had from existing customers 12 months ago, how much do you still have today, including expansion (upsells, upgrades) and net of churn (downgrades, lost customers). NRR above 100% means existing customers grew, even before adding new ones. Best-in-class SaaS sees NRR of 120%+; below 90% is concerning.

Example
Last April cohort: ₹2Cr ARR. Today (same customers): ₹2.3Cr after upsells of ₹50L and churn of ₹20L. NRR = 115%.
You'll see it
In every SaaS board pack, quarterly.
How to handle
NRR is more important than gross retention because it captures expansion. A SaaS company with 95% gross but 120% NRR is healthier than one with 100% gross and 100% NRR.
111

GRR Gross Revenue Retention

Same idea as NRR, but excluding expansion, measures pure leakage. Of the revenue from existing customers 12 months ago, how much remains today net of churn and downgrades, but ignoring upsells. GRR ≤ NRR by definition, and the gap shows you how much of the NRR story is expansion versus survival. Best-in-class SaaS targets 90%+ GRR.

Example
Last April cohort ₹2Cr; ₹20L churned; ₹50L expanded. GRR = 90% (₹1.8Cr / ₹2Cr). NRR = 115%. Gap is the expansion contribution.
You'll see it
In sophisticated investor diligence and product-led pricing reviews.
How to handle
Low GRR with high NRR is risky, you're depending on existing customers to keep buying more, which can mask product-market fit issues.
112

CAC Customer Acquisition Cost

The fully-loaded cost of acquiring a new customer, total sales + marketing spend in a period, divided by the number of new customers acquired in that period. Includes salaries of sales and marketing teams, ad spend, content costs, sales tools, partner commissions, and a portion of relevant overhead. The lower the CAC relative to LTV, the better the unit economics.

Example
Q3 sales + marketing spend ₹2Cr; 40 new customers signed in Q3. CAC = ₹5L per customer.
You'll see it
Every quarter in SaaS unit economics reviews.
How to handle
Be consistent about what's included. Excluding salaries makes CAC look better than it is; including overhead too aggressively does the opposite.
113

LTV Lifetime Value

The total gross profit a customer is expected to generate over their entire relationship with the company. Commonly approximated as ARPU × gross margin × (1 / churn rate) for SaaS. The LTV:CAC ratio is a fundamental health check, >3:1 is the rule-of-thumb good zone. Below 1:1 means you're paying more to acquire customers than they're worth.

Example
ARPU ₹6L/yr × 80% gross margin × (1 / 10% annual churn) = LTV ₹48L. CAC ₹5L → LTV:CAC = 9.6×. Healthy.
You'll see it
In every SaaS investor pitch.
How to handle
LTV is a model output, not a fact. The churn rate assumption drives most of the number, be conservative with it.
114

CAC Payback Period how fast CAC is recovered

The number of months it takes for the gross profit from a new customer to recover the CAC spent to acquire them. Calculated as CAC / (Monthly ARPU × Gross Margin). Best-in-class SaaS payback is < 12 months; 12-24 is acceptable; >24 months means cash is tied up for too long after each sale, which slows growth.

Example
CAC ₹5L; monthly ARPU ₹50K × 80% margin = ₹40K gross profit/month → payback = 12.5 months.
You'll see it
In every growth strategy discussion.
How to handle
If payback is stretching, the lever is usually pricing or efficiency, not just more sales hires.
115

Burn Rate monthly cash consumed

The net cash consumed by the business per month. Gross burn = total monthly cash outflow. Net burn = cash outflow minus cash inflow from revenue. For unprofitable startups, net burn is the more meaningful figure, it tells you how much cash the business actually loses per month after revenue. Burn is what determines runway.

Example
Monthly costs ₹80L; monthly revenue ₹50L. Net burn = ₹30L. Gross burn = ₹80L.
You'll see it
In every startup cash review.
How to handle
Burn measured over a single month is noisy. Use trailing 3-month average for planning decisions.
116

Runway months of cash remaining

The number of months the business can continue operating before running out of cash, calculated as Cash Balance / Monthly Net Burn. The single most important number at an unprofitable startup. Convention: when runway dips below 12 months, fundraising or cost-cutting becomes the dominant management priority. Below 6 months is crisis mode.

Example
Cash ₹6Cr; net burn ₹30L/month → runway = 20 months. Comfortable.
You'll see it
In every board pack and investor update at pre-profit companies.
How to handle
Always present runway alongside burn, burn alone is incomplete. Update runway every month using actual trailing burn, not the optimistic budget.
117

Bookings vs Revenue contracted vs recognized

Bookings = total contract value signed in a period (what the customer committed to pay). Revenue = the portion of that contract value recognized in the period per accounting rules. For a 1-year contract signed in January: full year value is January bookings; revenue is recognized 1/12th per month through the year. Confusing the two is one of the most common SaaS reporting mistakes.

Example
Signed a ₹24L annual contract on 15 March: March bookings = ₹24L; March revenue ≈ ₹1L (half a month).
You'll see it
In every sales review and revenue reconciliation.
How to handle
If someone says "we hit ₹2Cr this quarter", clarify which, bookings tell you sales momentum, revenue tells you P&L truth.
10

Live Scenarios

The terms make sense individually. Stitched together into a real ticket, they reveal what the work actually looks like. These seven scenarios are composites of things that happen at almost every finance team, every month. Read them through; you will see at least three of them in your first week.

SCENARIO 01: A vendor invoice for March was not booked until 5 April

MONTH-END
1

Spot the problem

You're closing March on the 4th. The AWS invoice for March (₹2.4L) usually arrives on the 2nd; this month it landed only on the 5th. The expense is March's, but you haven't booked it yet.

2

Decide the right entry

Accrual principle says: expense belongs to the period it was incurred (March), not paid (April). Book a March-end accrual: Dr AWS Hosting Expense ₹2.4L, Cr Accrued Expenses ₹2.4L. The ITC isn't claimed yet, the invoice formally hits books in April, when the GST flows through.

"Without this entry, March P&L understates hosting cost by ₹2.4L. Margin looks better than it is."
3

Reverse in April

On 5 April, when the invoice is formally booked: Dr Accrued Expenses ₹2.4L, Cr Vendor (AP) ₹2.4L (with separate Dr for ITC if booking the GST). The reversal cancels the accrual; the actual invoice now sits in AP awaiting payment.

4

What "good" looks like

You maintain a recurring accrual checklist for month-end, hosting, rent, utilities, subscription tools that bill in arrears, professional services not yet invoiced. Reduces the chance any predictable accrual is missed.

SCENARIO 02: A vendor's GST invoice doesn't appear in GSTR-2B

GST RECONCILIATION
1

Spot the gap

You're reconciling May purchase register to May GSTR-2B (downloaded on 14 June). Your books show ₹2.16L GST on an invoice from Vendor X. GSTR-2B shows zero from that vendor. Mismatch of ₹2.16L.

2

Diagnose the cause

Three possibilities: (a) vendor hasn't filed their GSTR-1 yet, (b) vendor filed with wrong GSTIN for you, (c) vendor filed in the wrong month.

"Pull the vendor's GST status from the portal, last return filed and date. Tells you which case you're in."
3

Take action

Email the vendor with the invoice number, your GSTIN, the period, and the missing ITC value. Ask them to file or amend. CC procurement so they're aware. Don't claim the ₹2.16L ITC in your GSTR-3B yet, claiming ITC not in 2B exposes the company to interest and notice.

4

Track until resolved

Maintain a "pending ITC" tracker per vendor. Once the missing entry appears in a later month's 2B, claim it then. Repeat-offender vendors should be flagged to procurement, non-compliance is a vendor risk, not just an accounting one.

SCENARIO 03: A key client has ₹35L sitting in 90+ days AR

COLLECTIONS
1

Surface the issue

Weekly AR aging review: Client ABC has ₹15L in 0-30, ₹10L in 31-60, and ₹35L in 90+. The 90+ has been there for over four months without movement.

2

Investigate before escalating

Pull invoice copies, delivery proofs, and the original PO. Check if there's a documented dispute or service issue. Look at communication history with the client's AP team, sometimes the invoice was rejected for a fixable reason (wrong PO reference, wrong GSTIN, missing supporting doc) and nobody knew.

"You'd be amazed how often 90+ days AR sits because of a ₹500 typo, not a payment refusal."
3

Escalate properly

If no fixable issue: draft a structured email to the client's finance contact summarizing each pending invoice (number, date, amount, PO ref). CC the relationship owner internally. If no response in 5 working days, escalate one level up. Document every contact attempt.

4

Provide for the worst case

If 120+ days with no concrete commitment, propose a bad debt provision in the next review. The provision doesn't write the AR off, it acknowledges the collection risk on the books. If collection eventually happens, the provision reverses. If not, the write-off is at least anticipated.

SCENARIO 04: Client deducted TDS but it's not in your Form 26AS

TDS RECONCILIATION
1

Spot the gap

You raised an invoice for ₹5L; client paid ₹4.5L (TDS ₹50K deducted under 194J). You expected to see ₹50K credited to your PAN in Form 26AS for that quarter. Q2 26AS is published, and the credit isn't there.

2

Confirm internally first

Cross-check: did the client share a payment advice mentioning TDS deduction? Was your PAN correctly mentioned on the invoice? Did the bank credit ₹4.5L (consistent with TDS) or the full ₹5L? Eliminate your-side issues before contacting the client.

3

Request Form 16A

Email the client's finance team requesting Form 16A for the quarter. If their TDS return was filed but with the wrong PAN, they need to file a correction return. If not filed at all, they need to file (with late fee, on their cost).

"Until 26AS shows it, you can't claim TDS credit in your ITR. Real cash leakage if not pursued."
4

Keep documentation

Save the client's payment advice, your invoice, the chase emails. If at ITR time the credit is still missing, you may need to file the ITR without the credit and pursue refund separately. Documentation is the only thing that lets you reconstruct the case.

SCENARIO 05: Auditor asks for supporting documents you don't have

AUDIT
1

The request

Statutory auditor is sampling Q3 expense vouchers. They've requested supporting for a ₹1.2L payment to "ABC Consultants", invoice, agreement, proof of services, TDS deducted, approval email. You can find the invoice in the system; nothing else.

2

Search exhaustively

Check shared drives, email archives, the original requester's inbox if accessible, the procurement file, the legal folder. Sometimes documents exist but were never centralized.

3

If genuinely missing: escalate and be honest

Don't fabricate or backdate. Flag to your manager immediately: "Audit has asked for X for ABC payment; only invoice available; agreement and approval not on record." Let leadership decide whether to get a fresh confirmation from the vendor and reconstruct what's possible, or to accept this as an audit observation.

"An audit observation is recoverable. A fabricated document is not. Don't ever confuse the two."
4

Fix the system

After audit closes, propose the process change that would have prevented this, e.g., "mandatory upload of agreement + approval email before any vendor payment above ₹50K is released." That's how a finance team matures.

SCENARIO 06: Employee submits ₹45K expense claim outside policy

REIMBURSEMENT
1

Read the claim carefully

Senior sales rep has submitted ₹45K, ₹30K hotel for a client visit (policy cap ₹15K/night, 2 nights), ₹10K business class flight (policy: economy only), ₹5K client dinner without prior approval (policy: pre-approval needed above ₹3K).

2

Don't reject in finance silence

Email the employee with a clear breakdown: which items align with policy, which are over, by how much, and what policy clause applies. Don't lecture, state facts. Offer two paths: (a) reduce claim to within policy; (b) get explicit written approval from the function head for the excess.

"Frame this as enforcement of an agreed rule, not as judgment of the employee. They didn't write the policy; you didn't pick this fight."
3

Process per their choice

If approval comes through with appropriate sign-off, process the full claim and tag it as "approved exception" in the audit trail. If they reduce, process the reduced amount. Don't process anything in between.

4

Watch for patterns

One employee with one exception is fine. Same person with monthly exceptions is a policy or behavior issue. Report patterns up, fixing them isn't finance's call, but spotting them is.

SCENARIO 07: Vendor demands payment before GRN is processed

PAYMENT CONTROL
1

The pressure

Vendor delivered laptops worth ₹6L on 20 March; invoice raised same day with "due immediately" terms. It's 25 March. Warehouse hasn't done the goods-in count yet (they're backlogged). Vendor's accounts team is calling daily.

2

Don't bypass the control

Three-way match exists for a reason, to prevent paying for things that weren't delivered, were short-delivered, or were defective. Once payment is made, leverage to fix a delivery issue drops to near-zero.

"Vendor urgency is not your urgency. The control exists precisely for this conversation."
3

Unblock the GRN

Escalate to the warehouse manager directly: "Need GRN for PO-2026-119 by EOD; vendor payment held pending." Most "we'll do it later" backlogs move within 24 hours of a named ask. Once GRN is processed, you can release payment cleanly.

4

Manage the relationship

Reply to the vendor: "Payment will be released by [date] post-GRN processing as per our standard procedure. Apologies for the delay." Don't promise specific timelines you can't control. Don't bypass procedure to "be nice", the next vendor will expect the same.
